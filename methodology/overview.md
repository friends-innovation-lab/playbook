# Public Impact Method

*A civic innovation methodology designed to move ideas into durable public outcomes.*

---

## The Promise

Progress that survives scrutiny, politics, and time.

---

## Why This Exists

In the public sector, failure is rarely a shortage of ideas. More often, it is the result of ideas that were never designed to survive the systems they were meant to change.

Innovation labs have introduced useful tools into government, but many of those efforts stall once pilots end or leadership shifts. Promising concepts struggle to move through policy constraints, procurement rules, and institutional incentives that were never accounted for at the outset.

The Public Impact Method was developed in response to that gap.

Rather than treating constraints as obstacles to work around, the method begins by examining them directly. It asks who holds authority, where risk accumulates, and how past efforts have shaped current conditions. Only then does it move toward solution design.

The method reflects a view that progress in public systems depends as much on legitimacy as it does on creativity. Ideas that move quickly but ignore political, legal, or operational realities often fail quietly. Those that acknowledge these forces early stand a better chance of lasting.

Public Impact Method emphasizes work that happens in real conditions, not controlled environments. Research is tied to decisions. Prototypes are shaped by policy and operations, not abstract possibility. Testing occurs where implementation would actually take place.

The final measure of success is not a demonstration or a pilot, but whether an idea can be carried forward by the institution itself. That requires clear ownership, alignment with existing processes, and an understanding of what it will take to maintain the work over time.

---

## The Philosophy

Four beliefs shape everything we do:

1. **Innovation in public systems must earn legitimacy, not just attention.**
   Flashy demos don't change institutions. Building trust with operators, leadership, and oversight does.

2. **Speed matters, but only when paired with risk awareness.**
   Moving fast without understanding constraints creates problems that take years to unwind.

3. **Good ideas fail more often from implementation debt than from lack of creativity.**
   The gap between "approved" and "operational" is where most innovation dies.

4. **The goal is institutional change, not prototypes.**
   A prototype is evidence. The real deliverable is a new way of working that outlasts the project team.

### 1. Ground

**Establish legitimacy and constraints before proposing solutions.**

Most projects fail before they start—by ignoring political reality, prior attempts, or who actually has decision authority. Ground prevents that.

**Focus Areas:**
- Policy, legal, and operational constraints
- Power, incentives, and decision authority
- Historical context and prior attempts
- What's been tried, what's been rejected, and why

**Outputs:**
- Constraint and opportunity map
- Stakeholder alignment snapshot
- Clear problem statement that names tradeoffs

**Why It Matters:**
This reassures people you're not here to break things blindly. You understand their world before asking them to change it.

---

### 2. Sense

**Develop shared understanding of users and systems.**

Research that doesn't drive decisions is theater. Sense focuses on understanding that directly shapes what we build and how we build it.

**Focus Areas:**
- Decision-driven research (what do we need to know to move forward?)
- Systems mapping and dependencies
- Second-order effects and equity impacts
- Who gains, who loses, and who decides

**Outputs:**
- System map showing dependencies and friction points
- Risk and impact register
- Insights translated into decision implications

**Why It Matters:**
This is where systems thinking shows up without jargon. We surface the connections that explain why "simple" solutions have failed before.

---

### 3. Shape

**Design viable pathways, not endless concepts.**

Innovation isn't about generating ideas. It's about narrowing to options that could actually work—given the constraints we've mapped.

**Focus Areas:**
- Bounded ideation (creativity within real limits)
- Policy-safe prototyping
- Tradeoff modeling (what do you gain vs. give up with each path?)

**Outputs:**
- Two to three viable solution paths
- Clear rationale for each option
- Recommendation with known risks

**Why It Matters:**
This shifts conversations from opinions to choices. Leadership doesn't want to brainstorm—they want to decide.

---

### 4. Test

**Validate in real conditions, not lab conditions.**

Demos are easy. Operations are hard. Test proves that something works where it actually has to work—with real users, real data, real constraints.

**Focus Areas:**
- Live-environment testing
- Operational readiness assessment
- Implementation rehearsal with actual operators

**Outputs:**
- Pilot findings with clear go/no-go criteria
- Implementation playbook
- Ownership and handoff plan

**Why It Matters:**
This is where credibility is earned or lost. A working prototype means nothing if it can't survive contact with reality.

---

### 5. Embed

**Ensure the work lasts beyond the project.**

This is the phase most innovation labs skip—and the one funders quietly care about most. What happens when the consultants leave?

**Focus Areas:**
- Institutional integration (who owns this going forward?)
- Budget, policy, and process alignment
- Knowledge transfer and stewardship
- Graceful sunset if the pilot shouldn't scale

**Outputs:**
- Governance and ownership model
- Sustainability plan or intentional sunset plan
- Measurement framework tied to public outcomes

**Why It Matters:**
Without Embed, you've built a science project. With it, you've built institutional change.
